The formula used to determine the speed and movement of the 'planets' were conservation of energy as well as momentum.

Let us denote the two masses of spheres as m1 and m2

Remember the formula for a volume of the sphere, 4πr3 / 3

Multiplying this by our density ρ, and we have our mass for each sphere based on its radius

Newton found that the force of gravity between two objects was inversely proportional to their distance squared, and directly proportional to their masses

We typically say the formula for the gravitational force is Gm1m2 / r2, where G is the universal Gravitational constant

When we take the integral of this to find the work done, we get that the portential energy as a function of distance is -Gm1m2 / r. Note the negative sign

Now we can begin setting up our energy conservation equation. When we insert the sphere, we know the starting distance, as well as the two masses of the spheres

Lets call our starting distance r0 and our distance at any point in time r

Next, we have to consider the kinetic energy of our 'planets', which can be represented by the formula mv2 / 2

We then get the formula -Gm1m2 / r0 = -Gm1m2 / r + m1v12 / 2 + m2v22 / 2, the formula is made easier since there is no starting velocity for either planet

Note that we know all these values except for v1 and v2, and this is where the conservation of momentum comes in.

The momentum, or often known as mv, is 0 at the start in the system since neither planet is moving. If we consider the system of planets as a whole, there is no change in total momentum since the gravity between the two planets is an internal force

We thus have the equation m1v1 + m2v2 = 0, leaving us with two equations and two unknowns

The actual values for v1 and v2 in terms of the other variables are not very nice, however, it can be noted that the velocity of either planet is approximately inversely proportional to the square root of their distance

This approximation becomes more and more accurate as the starting distance increases
